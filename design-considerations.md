## Introduction

W3C, as a non-profit organization, is driven by its [mission to make the Web work \- for everyone](https://www.w3.org/about/). That mission is supported by the more detailed directions set by the [W3C's Core Vision for the Web](https://www.w3.org/TR/w3c-vision/#vision-web) and the [Ethical Web Principles](https://www.w3.org/TR/ethical-web-principles/).

For the most part, these high-level principles get turned into concrete action plans through a mostly organic approach where the community identifies work that they expect will help accomplish that mission, and if these expectations are sufficiently widely shared, the work gets started and ideally does fulfill these expectations.

Over [W3C's 30+ years history](https://www.w3.org/about/history/), that approach has demonstrated its ability to deliver impressive results. But as the Web increase in complexity and in depth of integration in a growing number of people daily lives, there are gaps in what that organic approach allows to achieve:

* it makes it harder monitor if and measure how much the expectations of impact match the actual impact of these technologies, in the short-, mid- and long term evolution of the platform;  
* it risks missing critical needs to fulfill W3C's mission if the people with that need aren't positioned or prepared to build a proposal that match that need or to advocate for its prioritization;  
* more generally, it does not allow to have a structured, more strategic approach at identifying gaps, prioritizing actions and mitigations and working with other key actors of the internet ecosystem in coordinated efforts that maximize the impact of these distributed actions.

As part of the [W3C 2025 Roadmap to Develop a Strategy](https://www.w3.org/2025/06/w3c-2025-roadmap-to-develop-a-strategy/), W3C is running an [initiative to establish an Impact Framework](https://www.w3.org/Member/initiatives/impact-framework/) to help address these limitations.

Such an impact framework is meant to enable W3C to qualitatively and quantitatively define, analyze, and communicate its impact.

This document summarizes the methodology the task force preparing that framework has been taking to help the community and broader public to review and engage with its work.

## Theory of Change

An often used approach to connect activities from a non-profit organization to their intended long-term impact is the so-called [Theory of Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_change). It proposes a model that, starting from these long term impacts, asks what conditions are necessary for these impacts to realize; among these conditions, the subset of which the organization can realistically control or influence are then identified as the outcomes that organization should achieve, from which activities and input necessary to their accomplishment can be further derived.

All of the steps going from impact to activities and input are expected to be anchored as much as possible in robust evidence (e.g., scientific research), and verified over time through a series of indicators and metrics that help (1) strengthen effective programs and (2) identify gaps or mistakes in the logic chain in less effective ones.

As a result, a theory of change is expected to provide a dynamic model that serves both as a mirror showing how effective the organization is at achieving its goals, and a compass to guide its evolution.

The Impact Framework Task Force suggests adopting this model for W3C for the following reasons:

* it is broadly used in the non-profit world, making it easier for W3C to communicate and integrate with others in the sector, and providing the W3C community with a robust set of existing supporting materials and methodologies;  
* its logical and scientific anchoring makes it a compelling framework for W3C's community of technical contributors;  
* its grounding on long term impacts nicely complements W3C's well-honed bottom-up approach in building its roadmap and should address the structural gaps identified above.

## Defining W3C's Impact

Since a theory of change is derived from the impacts that are being sought, the first step in this process is to identify what impacts is W3C actually seeking to have on the world.

Fortunately, a lot of the work in articulating and building consensus on the high-level directions of W3C was done in recent years through the development and adoption as [W3C Statements](https://www.w3.org/standards/types/#STMT) of the [Ethical Web Principles](https://www.w3.org/TR/ethical-web-principles/) and the [W3C's Core Vision for the Web](https://www.w3.org/TR/w3c-vision/#vision-web) inside the the [Vision for W3C](https://www.w3.org/TR/w3c-vision/).

The Task Force thus proposes deriving W3C's impact statements from these documents. For the most part, these documents look at directions on a sought-after state of the Web. To make them suitable for a theory of change, the Task Force is re-articulating them in terms of how they would impact our target beneficiaries (which, since the Web is for everyone, is essentially humanity as a whole).

The [list of impact statements](impact-statements) is the result of that process, with some additional curation to reduce overlap across statements, to strengthen the need to keep the Web a competitive digital platform (as discussed in the context of the Vision) and to set aside (at least initially) those on which W3C seems unlikely to be well-positioned to bring influence. See Appendix A for more details on that curation process.

While this list of statements should be regularly monitored for relevance and correctness, the expectation is that once it has been established as a reasonable representation of W3C's impact, it should not need to change widely from year to year, since in most cases, actually contributing to these long term impacts requires long-term commitment and investment.

The Task Force also identified the following Impact Statements as important derivatives from the Vision and the Ethical Web Principles, but evaluated they were less directly relevant to W3C's scope given the limited influence it would likely have on their achievement:

* People understand how algorithms and data flows shape what they see. (derived from "The web is transparent", "The web makes it possible to verify information")
* People can participate in respectful, inclusive public discourse. (derived from "The web supports healthy community debate")
* People can build social, civic, and cultural connections online. (derived from "The web supports healthy community debate")
* People can participate in public conversations safely regardless of background. (derived from "The web supports healthy community debate")
